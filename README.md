task 5 Case Study: The QR Code "Payment Received" Scam
Description: This case study focuses on UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud, specifically the "Reverse QR Code" scam.
Scenario: A student lists an item for sale on an online marketplace. A "buyer" contacts them and claims they want to pay immediately. Instead of sending money, the scammer sends a QR code to the student via WhatsApp, claiming, "Scan this to receive the payment."
How it Happens: 
 1. The victim scans the QR code using a UPI app (like GPay or PhonePe).
2. The app prompts the victim to enter their UPI PIN.
3. The victim, thinking they are receiving money, enters the PIN.
4. In reality, the QR code was a "Collect Request." The moment the PIN is entered, money is deducted from the victim's account instead of being credited.
Consequences: Financial loss, emotional stress, and compromise of linked bank details. This scam targets young students and home-sellers who may be unfamiliar with how UPI request protocols work.

Stay Safe Online: Student Checklist
UPI Safety: Never enter your UPI PIN to receive money. A PIN is only required to send money.
Link Safety: Do not click on "Limited Time Offer" links sent via SMS or WhatsApp.
Public Wi-Fi: Avoid logging into bank accounts or VITyarthi using public railway or cafe Wi-Fi.
Strong Passwords: Use a mix of symbols, numbers, and cases (e.g., V!T_Bho_2026#).
Privacy: Do not share your OTP (One-Time Password) with anyone, even if they claim to be from your bank.
Software Updates: Keep your phone's OS and apps updated to patch security holes.
Identity Theft: Never share photos of your Aadhaar card or College ID on public social media.
Reporting: If scammed, immediately report it to the National Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) or call the Helpline 1930.







  
 



Cybercrime Awareness Case Study & Prevention Guide
Research & Surprises:
While researching UPI fraud for this task, I was most surprised to learn how psychologically manipulative scammers can be. They often create a "sense of urgency" to stop the victim from thinking clearly. I realized that even technically-inclined students can fall victim if they don't understand the specific "Collect Request" mechanism of UPI apps. It was eye-opening to see that the National Cyber Crime Portal and the 1930 helpline are available 24/7 to assist victims in India.
Habit Change:
As a result of this research, I have decided to change my habit of using the same password across multiple platforms. I have now implemented a password manager and enabled Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on my primary academic and financial accounts. As a Digital Ambassador, I will emphasize to my peers that "Security is a process, not a product."
